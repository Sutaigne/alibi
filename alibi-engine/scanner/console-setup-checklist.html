Alibi · Console Companion

Console setup checklist

A photo and screenshot dossier you produce yourself, so a reviewer can see exactly what your console gaming rig looks like — without having to take your word for it.

When to use this checklist
Use this only if there is no PC connected to your console rig. If a PC is in the loop (capture card host, secondary monitor, streaming PC, shared desktop), run console-run-check.bat on that PC instead — it produces stronger evidence automatically.
Required Skip these and the dossier is incomplete.
Recommended Strengthens the dossier; not strictly required.
Optional Useful for specific high-stakes scenarios.
Part A · The console itself

Show every port and every device that's plugged in

A console-MITM cheat stack requires something physically connected to a USB port: a controller adapter, a capture card pass-through, or a HID-emulating box. Photos of every port on the console make this visible. Take them in good light so cables and adapters are identifiable.

01

Console, front view Required

One clear photo of the front of the console. All USB ports must be visible. Any devices plugged in must be visible.

01_console-front.jpg

Why: the front USB ports are where input-adapter dongles (XIM, Cronus, ReaSnow) are most commonly plugged in.

02

Console, back view Required

One clear photo of the back of the console with all cables visible. HDMI, power, ethernet, any USB or accessory port should be in frame.

02_console-back.jpg

Why: capture-card pass-through setups are wired through the back HDMI port. An unfamiliar HDMI device between the console and the TV is the giveaway.

03

HDMI cable trace Required

Photo following the HDMI cable from the back of the console to the TV. Capture every device the cable passes through. If it goes straight from console to TV, that photo proves it.

03_hdmi-trace.jpg

Why: this is the single most important photo for ruling out a capture-card MITM rig. The cheat requires the video signal to be intercepted before it reaches the TV.

04

Wider rig shot Recommended

One photo from a few feet back showing the whole gaming area: console, TV, any PCs, monitors, cabling, peripherals.

04_full-rig.jpg

Why: close-ups can hide neighboring equipment. A wide shot proves there isn't a second PC or monitor just out of frame.

Part B · Console settings screenshots

What the console itself reports

Most consoles let you screenshot the system UI. Take screenshots of the screens listed below using the console's built-in screenshot button (PlayStation: Create button, Xbox: Share button, Switch: Capture button). Then transfer them off the console via the system's screenshot-sharing feature, USB stick, or by sending them to a phone via the official app.

05

Accessories / Devices menu Required

Screenshot of the system Settings screen that lists connected accessories. On PlayStation: Settings → Accessories. On Xbox: Settings → Devices & connections → Accessories. Include all paired controllers and any other USB device.

05_accessories.png

Why: this lists every input device the console recognizes. A Cronus or XIM adapter shows up here with a specific name (or as an unrecognized device, which is also evidence).

06

Controller test screen Required

Screenshot of the in-system controller test or controller information screen with each controller you use, individually. Capture the firmware version and the input source name. On PlayStation: Settings → Accessories → Controller (General) → Communication Method and similar sub-screens. On Xbox: Devices & connections → Accessories → (controller name).

06_controller_a.png, 06_controller_b.png, ...

Why: a genuine first-party controller reports specific firmware versions and identifiers. Adapters and emulators can be detected here.

07

Linked accounts Recommended

Screenshot of the Settings screen showing linked third-party accounts (Twitch, Discord, YouTube, etc.). On PlayStation: Settings → Users and Accounts → Link with Other Services. On Xbox: Account → Linked social accounts.

07_linked-accounts.png

Why: streaming/recording linked accounts (Twitch + Discord) are normal. The absence of unusual third-party services is one more confirming signal.

08

Storage / installed games Recommended

Screenshot of the system storage screen showing installed games and apps. PlayStation: Settings → Storage → Console Storage → Games and Apps. Xbox: My games & apps.

08_installed-games.png

Why: no third-party "cheat app" can be installed on a modern console - the OS won't allow it. But this screenshot proves the installed-software list contains only signed first-party titles.

Part C · The controller itself

Photos of the controller you actually play with

Some console cheating involves modified controllers: scuf-style rear paddles with built-in macro chips, or aftermarket boards inserted under the back plate. Photos of each controller you compete with - inside and out - rule this out.

09

Controller exterior Required

Photos of the front, back, top (triggers), and bottom of each controller you use competitively. Include any rear paddles or attachments.

09_controller_a_exterior.jpg

Why: an off-brand or modified controller may be obvious from the outside. A genuine first-party controller has identifiable manufacturer markings and serial number.

10

Controller interior Optional

If a high-stakes competitive context requires it, open the controller and photograph the internal PCB. A genuine first-party board has the manufacturer logo and standard component layout. Modchips and macro boards are visually distinct from anything original.

10_controller_a_interior.jpg

Why: internal controller modifications (macro chips, rapid-fire boards) can be invisible from the outside but are immediately obvious when the case is opened. Only do this if asked, since opening the controller may void warranty.

Part D · Live verification (optional, highest credibility)

The reviewer watches you

For the highest level of verification, a reviewer joins a real-time video call and watches you go through the checklist live. Static photos can be reused or staged. A live walkthrough cannot.

11

Live video walkthrough Optional

Join a video call (Discord, Zoom, whatever the reviewer prefers). Walk through items 01-10 live, on camera, with the reviewer asking questions. Show the console booting, controllers being picked up, the settings screens being navigated.

Why: this catches what photos cannot — controllers swapped after a photo was taken, adapters hidden between the wall and the console, settings changes made between screenshots. It is the gold standard if both parties have the time.

Best practices

How to make this dossier actually credible

Make your evidence harder to fake

  • Take all the photos in one continuous session, on the same day. Most cameras embed a timestamp in the file metadata.
  • Include something in each photo that changes day-to-day — a piece of paper with today's date written on it, a phone showing the date, a TV showing today's news. This stops old photos from being recycled.
  • Use the original phone-camera files (no edits, no filters). Save as .jpg or .heic, do not re-export through editing apps.
  • Send the dossier as a single ZIP file with the filenames listed above, so the reviewer can scan it in order.
  • If a reviewer asks for a specific extra photo you didn't take, take it. Refusing to provide a specific shot reads worse than providing it.

What this checklist cannot prove

A determined cheater can stage some of this. Photos of a clean rig can be taken the day before a cheating session begins. A capture card can be unplugged for the photo and reconnected later. A modified controller can be swapped after the photo for an unmodified one.

This is why live verification (item 11) is qualitatively stronger than static photos, and why scanning a connected PC (the .ps1 scan) is qualitatively stronger than this checklist. Run the scanner if a PC is available.

The honest framing for a reviewer: this dossier raises the cost of cheating undetected. It does not lower it to zero.
